The Great Legend

One of Humanity's Greatest Stories

The Mahābhārata is one of the two major Sanskrit epics of ancient India, attributed to the sage Vyāsa. It narrates the Kurukshetra War and the fates of the Kaurava and Pāndava princes.

Beyond its narrative, the epic contains philosophical and devotional material, including the Bhagavad Gītā, the Vishnu Sahasranāma, and discussions of dharma, artha, kāma, and moksha — the four aims of Hindu life.

It is the longest epic poem ever written, containing over 200,000 individual verse lines (shlokas) or about 1.8 million words total.

Heroes & Legends

Key Characters

Pāṇḍava
Arjuna
The Peerless Archer · Third Pāṇḍava

The third Pāṇḍava prince, renowned as the greatest archer of his age. Son of Indra, student of Droṇa, and the charioteer of Lord Krishna in the Bhagavad Gītā.

"O Kṛṣṇa, I see my kinsmen arrayed for battle, and my limbs quail, my mouth goes dry." — Bhagavad Gītā I.28
Avatar of Vishnu
Krishna
The Divine Charioteer · Guide of Dharma

The eighth avatar of Vishnu, friend and guide to the Pāṇḍavas. He served as Arjuna's charioteer and revealed the eternal truths of the Bhagavad Gītā.

"You have a right to perform your prescribed duties, but you are not entitled to the fruits of your actions." — Bhagavad Gītā II.47
Kaurava Ally
Karṇa
Tragic Warrior · Son of Sūrya

Born to Kuntī and the sun god Sūrya, Karṇa became the greatest rival of Arjuna. Renowned for his generosity and loyalty, his tragedy is one of the epic's most moving stories.

"I will not be conquered by death itself — but I bow to fate alone."
Pāṇḍava · Eldest
Yudhiṣṭhira
King of Dharma · Eldest Pāṇḍava

The eldest Pāṇḍava, son of Dharma himself. Known for his unwavering commitment to truth and righteousness, he became the emperor of Hastināpura after the war.

"Dharma alone is the eternal refuge of a man, and there is no refuge beyond that."
Pāṇḍava · Second
Bhīma
The Wind-Strong · Master of the Mace

Second Pāṇḍava, son of Vāyu the wind god. Possessing the strength of ten thousand elephants, he was the greatest mace-fighter of his era and slew 99 of the 100 Kauravas.

"My arms are my weapons, and I shall rip apart any who stand against dharma."
Kaurava Commander
Droṇa
Guru of Warriors · Supreme Archer

The royal teacher of both Pāṇḍavas and Kauravas in martial arts. Droṇāchārya was the supreme archer-sage whose prowess was unmatched, serving as Commander of the Kaurava forces.

"A true teacher lives through the greatness of his students."
Grandsire · First Commander
Bhīṣma
The Terrible Vow · Ichamrityu

Born Devavrata, he renounced the throne and swore celibacy — earning the name Bhīṣma. He possessed the rare boon of choosing his own moment of death and fell on the 10th day of battle.

"I who am celibate, I who lie on the bed of arrows, I have seen dharma today as never before."
Pāṇḍava Queen
Draupadī
Born of Fire · Queen of the Pāṇḍavas

Daughter of King Drupada, born from a sacred fire. Wife of all five Pāṇḍavas, her humiliation at the dice game ignited the great war. Her unbound hair was a vow until vengeance was complete.

"Even the gods cannot protect those who have abandoned their own dignity."
Author & Sage
Vyāsa
Composer of the Epic · Omniscient Sage

Krishna Dvaipāyana Vyāsa is both the author of the Mahābhārata and a character within it. Grandson of Vasiṣṭha, father of Dhṛtarāṣṭra and Pāṇḍu — he dictated the epic to Gaṇeśa.

"I have composed this great work which is both dharma and artha and kāma and moksha."
The Decisive War

The Battle of Kurukshetra

For eighteen days on the plains of Kurukshetra, the armies clashed in history's most celebrated war — a conflict over the throne of Hastināpura and the very soul of dharma.

Key Moments of the 18-Day War
The Conch Calls War
Krishna blows the Pāñcajanya, Arjuna the Devadatta. The earth trembles as 18 akshauhinis arrayed face to face. Arjuna's despair gives birth to the Bhagavad Gītā.
⟶ Arjuna · Krishna
Day
1
Bhīṣma Commands
Bhīṣma, the mighty grandsire, takes command of the Kaurava army. His prowess on the field is unmatched — the Pāṇḍavas struggle to contain his devastating assault.
⟶ Bhīṣma · Duryodhana
Bhīṣma Falls
Arjuna, guided by Krishna, brings down the invincible Bhīṣma using Śikhaṇḍī as a shield. The grandsire falls on a bed of arrows, choosing to wait for the auspicious moment of death.
⟶ Arjuna · Śikhaṇḍī · Bhīṣma
Day
10
Chakravyūha — Abhimanyu's Last Stand
Droṇa forms the deadly spiral formation Chakravyūha. Abhimanyu, son of Arjuna, penetrates it alone but is surrounded and slain by six Maharathis simultaneously — an act condemned as adharma.
⟶ Abhimanyu · Droṇa · Karṇa
Day
13
Arjuna's Terrible Oath
Learning of his son's death, Arjuna swears to kill Jayadratha before sunset or enter the fire. With Krishna's divine intervention and brilliance, Arjuna fulfils his vow — the sun briefly hides.
⟶ Arjuna · Jayadratha
Droṇa Slain
Droṇāchārya, upon hearing the (deceptive) news of Ashwatthama's death, lays down his arms in grief. Dhrishtadyumna beheads the greatest teacher — fulfilling a long-sworn vow of revenge.
⟶ Dhṛṣṭadyumna · Droṇa
Day
15
The Death of Karṇa
In a clash of titans, Karṇa's chariot wheel sinks in the earth. Arjuna, instructed by Krishna, shoots the fatal arrow while Karṇa is unarmed — fulfilling destiny and breaking two hearts.
⟶ Arjuna · Karṇa · Krishna
Day
17
Duryodhana's Last Stand
The final Kaurava, Duryodhana, hides in a lake. Goaded out, he fights Bhīma in a duel of maces. Bhīma strikes the fatal blow on his thigh — ending the war as the sun sets on Day 18.
⟶ Bhīma · Duryodhana

Bhagavad Gītā

The Song of God · 18 Chapters · 700 Verses

Never was there a time when I did not exist, nor you, nor all these kings; nor in the future shall any of us cease to be. As the embodied soul continuously passes, in this body, from boyhood to youth to old age, the soul similarly passes into another body at death. A sober person is not bewildered by such a change.

— Bhagavad Gītā 2.12–13
